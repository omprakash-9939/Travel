# Problem Statements: Worked Examples (travel technology)

Read these when a problem feels vague, bundled, or solution-led. Each pair shows a weak framing and a stronger one, in a travel-tech context.

## Example 1 — Solution disguised as a problem

**Weak:** "We need a fare-comparison widget."
*Why it's weak: names a solution, no problem, no evidence, no measure.*

**Stronger:** "Leisure travellers searching multi-airline routes abandon before booking 64% of the time, and exit surveys cite 'couldn't tell which fare was the best value'. Success means cutting search-to-book abandonment on multi-airline routes to under 50%."
*A comparison widget may well be the answer — but now it's one option judged against a real problem.*

## Example 2 — Vague and unmeasurable

**Weak:** "Our booking conversion is low."
*Why it's weak: "low" is undefined and the funnel isn't located.*

**Stronger:** "Search-to-book conversion is 1.8% against a 3% benchmark, and 70% of the drop happens between fare selection and payment. Success means lifting payment-step completion from 40% to 60%."

## Example 3 — Bundled problems

**Weak:** "The booking flow is slow and confusing, we lose customers, and the call centre is swamped."
*Why it's weak: several problems in one; you cannot solve or measure them together.*

**Stronger:** Split into separate, linked statements, then prioritise:
- "The fare-quote step takes 8s (target 2s), and slow quotes correlate with a 15-point drop in completion."
- "32% of booking-related support calls are about seat-selection confusion."
Treat each as its own problem; they may share a root cause (find out in `root-cause.md`).

## Example 4 — Blaming people instead of describing the problem

**Weak:** "Agents keep booking the wrong fares."
*Why it's weak: assigns blame, presumes the cause, invites a process-policing 'solution'.*

**Stronger:** "On 18% of agent bookings the fare shown at quote differs from the fare at confirmation, causing rebooking and refunds averaging £40k/month. Success means fare-at-quote matching fare-at-confirm on 99%+ of bookings."
*Now root-cause analysis can ask why they diverge — usually a stale-availability or re-pricing issue, not an agent one.*

## The test
Neutral about solution? Has a number and a target? One problem? Backed by evidence (or a flagged assumption)? Bounded with an out-of-scope? If all yes, it's ready.
